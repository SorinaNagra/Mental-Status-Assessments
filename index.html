Mental Status Assessments

The mental status assessment incorporates subjective client data thought self- report and the clinician’s objective observations to describe the client’s current mental state. This is used to establish a baseline, evaluate change and efficacy of treatment. (Gatkowski, 2014)

Appearance and Behaviour

Appearance:

Appearance is a physical description of the client based on observations

  • Do they look their stated age?
    • Older: may be do to medical conditions, substance abuse or uncontrolled mental illness (Voss & Das, 2020)
    • Youthful may be due to developmental delay or age- inappropriate dress
  • Clothing: what the individual is wearing, appropriateness and condition of clothing
    • Inappropriate attire indicative of disorientation (Gatkowski, 2014)
    • Are they dressed in an age appropriate manner (Voss & Das, 2020)
  • Build: is the individual thin, obese, athletic or medium build
  • Grooming and hygiene is indicative of a person’s functioning and ability to perform ADLs (Voss & Dass, 2020)
    • malodorous, unkempt, dirty
    • Appropriate
    • Adequate
    • Disheveled: untidy or messy
      • Indicative of depression, neurocognitive impairment, negative symptoms of schizophrenia
    • Poor hygiene indicative of poor functioning (Voss & Das, 2020)
  • Distinguishing features (Voss & Dass, 2020: RNAO, 2015)
    • tattoos or scars: May be indicative of personality, history or behaviour (Voss & Das, 2020)
    • Scars: old/ significant injury, accidental trauma, harm caused by another individual, or self- induced harm
    • Self- inflicted injury: superficial cutting or injury from past suicide attmepts
    • needle tracks drug use

Behaviour

Description of the patients manner during the interview (Voss & Das, 2020)

  • Eye contact

    • Good
    • Fleeting
    • None

  • Psychomotor

    • Agitation/ hyperkinesia
    • Changes in behaviour: combative attitude, tachycardia, sweating, tremor, repetitive movements (Vieta et al, 2017)
    • Restlessness, pacing, shaking, fidgeting (Wong & Leventhal, 2015)
    • Wringing of hands, inability to sit still (Norris et al, 2016)
    • Take not of any tics: sudden, repetitive movement of eyes, extremity or trunk (Gatkowski, 2014)

  • Retardation: slow movements (RNAO, 2015)

  • Catatonia: immobility/ muscle rigidity (Norris et al, 2016)

    • Note waxy flexibility: a person will maintain a posture for a long period of time after being molded into it. (Gatkowski, 2014)

  • Parkinsonism: pill-rolling, Bradykinesia, rigidity (Voss & Das, 2020)

  • extrapyramidal side effects (EPS) from antipsychotics are rigidity, tremors, and tics such as teeth grinding, lip-smacking, or tongue protrusions (Voss & Das, 2020)

  • Acute dystonic reaction sudden rigidity after antipsychotic administrationL life threatening if involving the laryngeal muscles (Voss & Das, 2020)

  • Disheveled, indicate self- neglect or preoccupation with other influence (Gatkowski, 2014)

  • Slouched posturing + downcast affect may indicate depression

  • Agitation indicative of anxiety or hypomania

  • Attitude towards interviewer (2009)

    • Pleasant
    • Engaged
    • Open
    • Guarded: offered little during interaction
    • Suspicious
    • Hostile/ irritability can occur with anxiety
    • May present with underlying irritability: always present

  • Description of interaction

    • Cooperative
    • Avoidant
    • Selectively mute
    • Unable to redirect, may be acutely responding to internal stimuli or may be indicative of manic symptoms (Voss & Das, 2020)

Distress and discomfort may be due to medical or mental illness or indicate the circumstances if the patient was brought to the hospital against their will (Voss & Das, 2020)

Note: monitor for gestures: head tilting, glancing, waving that may indicate client is internally preoccupied and responding to internal stimuli (Gatkowski, 2014)

  • Is the behaviour appropriate for the situation? (Voss & Das, 2020)
    • Involuntary admissions may be uncooperative
    • Unusual if the patient is smiling and laughing in this situation

Mood and Affect

Mood

  • Mood is a subjective report of the client’s their emotional state (Voss & Das, 2020)
    • appropriate/ inappropriate for the situation
    • Euthymic: normal rage in mood: absence of depressive or elevated mood
    • Euphoric/ elated
    • Dysphoric: negative mood, uncomfortable, unhappiness, dissatisfied with self of uneasy (Gatkowski, 2014)
  • How is your mood? Can you elaborate? Can you describe your day?
  • Rate the person’s mood out of 10, with 10 being the greatest

Affect

  • A description of the clients expression (Gatkowski, 2014; RNAO, 2017)
    • Congruent: consistent with expressed thoughts vs. incongruent
    • Full rage: emotions are appropriately expressed
    • Constricted: little variation in expression
    • Flat : absence of expression
    • blunted : limited expression
    • Labile: rapid/ abrupt changes in range or expressed emotions
    • Stable: absence of rapid changes
    • Appropriate: consistent with their mood and accompanying ideas

Speech

  • Verbalization: the amount someone speaks (Norris et al, 2016)
    • Poverty of speech
    • Hyperverbal
  • Fluency
    • Indicative of language skills or a person’s ability to understand/ generate speech
  • Rate
    • Slow vs. pressured (Voss & Das, 2020)
    • Response time
      • Long pauses before answering questions (RNAO, 2017)
      • Hesitant (RNAO, 2017)
  • Rhythm
    • Monotone (RNAO, 2017)
    • Stuttering (RNAO, 2017)
  • Volume
    • Quiet: depressed/ withdrawn (Voss & Das, 2020)
    • Loud: agitated (Voss & Das, 2020)
  • Tone
    • Aggressive, intensity, child- like (Voss & Das, 2020)
  • Articulation (clear, mumbled, slurred)(RNAO, 2017)
  • Are they able to make their needs known?

Thought and perception

Form involves the flow of ideas

Content involves describing the person’s ideas or what a person thinks: includes delusions. Subjective data, content of thoughts as determined by the client’s oral report (Voss & Das, 2020)

Document topic*

Suicidal or homicidal ideations (intent, plan, access to means, time-frame) (RNAO, 2017)* Talk more about this under risk assessment

Delusions: false fixed belief (RNAO, 2017) On admission, the client’s recollection of events need to be validated through another person, often a close family member who can confirm or contradict beliefs.

  • Delusions of control: belief that one’s thoughts or feelings are influenced by an utaize force (Gatkowski, 2014)
    • Thought insertion: belief implanted into one’s head by an external force (TV, another person, etc)(Gatkowski, 2014)
      • Withdrawal: belief someone or something is taking their thoughts away.
    • Thought broadcasting: belief that others can know one’s thoughts (Gatkowski, 2014)
    • Ideas of reference: belief of personal involvement in external event (Belief that a person on television is talking to them/ about them) (RNAO, 2017)
    • Does the radio or television speak to you? ()
  • Paranoid delusions: irrational distrust or fear that someone intends to harm them
    • Are you worried someone had been following or spying on you? (Voss & Das, 2020)
  • Persecutory delusions: belief that one is being harmed in some way. Watched, manipulated, discriminate against, etc.
  • Bizarre delusions/ implausible beliefs: “A rat ran across the floor and up into my stomach” “the light communicates with me”
  • Somatic delusions: false belief about bodily functions (Gatkowski, 2014)
    • Common: belief that they are pregnant, in reality are bloated or constipated, common in both males and females
  • Delusions of grandeur: belief that one holds power or influence
    • Ask about occupation or influence
  • Religious delusions: belief they are being spoken to by god, are an agent of a greater being (Gatkowski, 2014)
  • Nihilism: belief that one is dead/ non- existent(Gatkowski, 2014)
  • Erotomania: belief that a public figure or other person unknown to the individual is in a relationship with them (Gatkowski, 2014)

(Norris et al, 2016) * Do you have images in your head you cannot get out? * Do you have intense or irrational fears? * Do you think people are trying to hurt you in some way? Can you specify who? * Have you ever heard things others have not?

  • Depressive cognition: guilt/ hopelessness (RNAO, 2017)

  • Obsession: unwanted recurring thoughts (RNAO, 2017)

  • Phobia: Strong/ persistent fear (RNAO, 2017)

  • Thought process: a description of how the patient thinks or the organization of their thoughts (Voss & Das, 2020)

    • Normal: linear/ goal oriented (Voss & Das, 2020)
    • Loosening of association: no logical relationship between ideas, jumps between unconnected topics (Gatkowski, 2014)
    • Circumstantial: includes indirect/ unnecessary thoughts and details but eventually answers the question/ returns to original topic (Gatkowski, 2014)
    • Tangential: person moves away form the topic and does not answer the original question/ Return to main topic(Gatkowski, 2014; Voss & Das, 2020 )
    • Thought blocking: pause/ disturbance in one’s train of thought. (Gatkowski, 2014)
    • Flight of ideas: frequent shifts between topics, rapid verbalization
    • Loose/ disorganized: no train of thought to follow (Voss & Das, 2020)
    • Word salad: incoherent mixture of words and phrases (Gatkowski, 2014)v
    • Echolalia: repetition of words or phrases said by others (Gatkowski, 2014)
    • Pressured speech: increased rate and volume of speech, difficult to interrupt (Gatkowski, 2014)
    • Poverty of thought: provides little or vague responses during assessment. Lack of elaboration of ideas.
    • Preservation: Patient returns to the same topic no matter the question (Voss & Das, 2020)
      • document topic associated with type of thought
    • Incoherence: unintelligible speech
    • Rumination: repetitive and passive focus
      • I.e Rumination of hopelessness (Michl et al, 2013)

Perception

Descriptions of one’s experience, the client’s description of their perception of sensory stimuli or lack thereof.

  • Hallucinations: interpretation/ experience of sensory perception in the absence of external stimuli
  • Can be auditory, visual, tactile, olfactory or gustatory
    • Have patients describe their experience and test if they can distinguish between reality and hallucination
    • Command hallucinations: person perceives a command that the individual feels obligated to obey
    • Have you heard or seen something others don’t seem to? ()
    • If they hear voices
      • Do they recognize the voices? How many?
      • What are the voices telling them?
      • Describe your experience?
  • Does the client appear internally preoccupied?
    • Responding to internal stimuli: laughing to self, speaking to self, etc.
  • Illusion: misinterpretation of real stimuli
    • Thinking that the wind is a voice calling them, or that a piece of clothing is an insect Pressured speech: increased rate and volume of speech, difficult to interrupt (Gatkowski, 2014)

Risk assessment

Risk of self harm, violence against others or suicide.

  • suicidal / homicidal ideation:
    • Does the person have thoughts, intent or plans to harm themselves or others
    • Passive suicidal thoughts: has thoughts about death, no intent or plan to end their life
    • Active: plans or intent to end their own life, significantly increased risk (Smith et al, 2019)
    • Have you had thoughts of hurting yourself?
    • Have you attempted to harm yourself in the past?
    • Have you thought you may actually hurt yourself now? Do you have a plan to?
    • There’s a big difference between having a thought and acting on it? Do you think you’re going to hurt yourself now/ soon?
    • Is there anything that prevents you from harming yourself?
  • Homicidal ideation:
    • Do you feel wronged by anyone?
    • Are you angry at anyone right now? What would you do if they were here?
    • Do they wish someone was dead? Or that the world/ their life would be better off without someone? (Voss & Das, 2020)

Cognition

  • Orientation: person place time
    • Normal alert and oriented x3
  • Level of consciousness (LOC): (Voss & Das, 2020)
    • Alert
    • Somnolent: Drowsy LOC, easily roused/ perceive stimuli)
    • Obtunded: Delayed response, no perception mild stimuli)
    • Stupor: aroused by intense or noxious stimuli and fall without continued stimuli
    • Comatose: unresponsive
  • Comment on their ability to concentrate on the conversation/ activities
  • Comment on their attention span on tasks or activities
    • May document their performance as good, limited, fair or poor (Voss & Das, 2020)
  • Memory
    • Remote / recent (last 24 hours) memory
    • Do they remember events leading to their admission?
    • Do they remember events from earlier today?
    • Do they remember meeting you the day before?
    • Do they remember conversations/ phone calls from earlier that day?
    • Unrelated words test: recall of phrase or random words/ numbers after another activity (at 1 and 5 mins) to avoid repetition/ practice (Voss & Das, 2020)
  • Insight into illness: does the client understand that they present with an illness and their functionality, non- delusional. (Gatkowski, 2014; Voss & Das, 2020)
    • Identify auditory hallucinations as not real = good insight (Voss & Das, 2020)
    • Patient does not realize they are experiencing paranoia about persecutory thoughts, insight is poor (Voss & Das, 2020)
  • Judgment: comment on the person’s ability to make logical decisions or think about options.
    • Can be poor, limited, fair or good (Voss & Das, 2020)
    • Abnormal findings may impair their own safety or the safety of others
    • Poor judgment: refusal to take medications (Voss & Das, 2020)
    • Intrusive with co-patients - poor judgment
    • Being unable to think of possible options may lead to suicidal ideation or may involve ruminating hopelessness

Presentations

  • Bipolar type 1
  • Psychosis
  • Anxiety
  • Mania
  • Adjustment disorder
  • Personality disorder
    • Cluster A includes paranoid, schizoid, and schizotypal.
      • present: suspicious, reclusive an odd-> eccentric personality
      • paranoid: distrustful
      • schizoids: reclusive, find difficulty in forming relationships, minimal sex drives, indifferent to praise or critique, blunted affect
      • schizotypal: traits overlapping with schizophrenia: “bizarre behavior/speech/thought content, inappropriate affect, magical beliefs, and abnormal visual experiences” - often family history of schizophrenia or personality disorder (Fariba et al, 2020)
    • Cluster B includes antisocial, borderline, histrionic, and narcissistic.
      • dramatic
      • violate social norms: often fall victim to impulse-> crime. Are volatile, act out, illogical temper tantrums/ act out
    • antisocial personality disorder: abnormal childhood behavior-> physical alterations peers/ adults, arson, animal cruelty, conduct diorder* -> reckless, * aggressive, may go by an alias-> pathological liars
      • Characterized by failure to conform to legal and social norms-> manipulativeness and infringing on the rights of others
    • Borderline personality disorder: mood instability, unstable untense relationships, uncontrollable anger, identity disturbances, fear of abandonment, self harm and chronic suicidal ideation - more then ¾ engage in self harm
      • Periods of spacing out/ dissociation during heightened stress
      • Morphology of histrionic personality disorder comes from hyderia-> associated with conversion disorder
      • Histoeronic patients- attention seeking, overly concerned with physical appearance, attempt to be the centered of attention, difficulty of maintaining relationships, sexually promiscuous
    • narcissistic personality disorder: self- centered, egotistical grandiosity
      • Lack empathy, overly sensitive to evaluation from others, exaggerate accomplishments, display a sense of entitlement
      • Irritating and haughty (arrogantly superior or disdainful) and seek attention when they feel unfairly deprived or experiencing a narcissistic injury (Fariba et al, 2020)
    • Cluster C includes avoidant, dependent, obsessive-compulsive
      • anxiety, abnormal fears, desire for untenable social relationships
        • Avoidant personality disorder-> low self extreme,, overt sensitivity to criticism, feeling sof social inadequacy- hesitate from social relationships
      • dependant personality disorder- depend on others for emotional validation
      • Psychoanalysis perceive this state arises from a failure to progress through the oral stage of development
      • Obsessive compulsive personality disorder: perfectionists-> inflexible, overly conscientious and display mildly constricted affect
      • More prevalent in higher echelons of education and income (Fariba et al, 2020)
    • Evaluation of personality disorders: non- intrusively, general questions that will not create a defensive/ hostile environment
      • Lack of insight- collect data from accounts to those closest to patient -> collateral best collected from police, family, parole officers
      • Treatment:
        • lack insight- will not see behavior as being maladaptive- egosyntonic (consistent with one’s self concept-> feel problem is with everyone else not self)
        • no medications approved to treat
        • cluster a:
          • group therapy may not be a good choice, suspicious and distrustful
          • Individual social skills training
          • Augmentation with second generation antipsychotics for schizoid and schizotypal types
        • Cluster B
          • Benefit from group therapy
          • Antisocial patients: reduced aggression main target - pharmacologic agents: lithium, valproic acid, and antipsychotics
          • Histrionic patients benefit CBT-> focus need for attention
          • Narcissistic patients uniquely difficult, challenge and critique all therapy -> ideally benefit from intensive psychodynamic psychotherapy
          • Dialectical behavior therapy - DBT - is effective for borderline
        • cluster C
          • CBT to address assertiveness, independence, attitude along with SSRI to address anxiety
          • Avoidant personality disorder- Supportive psychotherapy, assertiveness, social skills training and psychodynamic psychotherapy
          • obsessive compulsive personality disorder: psychoanalytic psychotherapy (Fariba et al, 2020)
